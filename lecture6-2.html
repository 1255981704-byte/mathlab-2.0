Sum and Difference Rules

STATEMENT

If F is an antiderivative of f and G is an antiderivative of g, then their sum is an antiderivative of f + g.

SUM RULE
\[\displaystyle \boxed{\int(f(x)+g(x))\,dx=\int f(x)\,dx+\int g(x)\,dx}\]
DIFFERENCE RULE
\[\displaystyle \boxed{\int(f(x)-g(x))\,dx=\int f(x)\,dx-\int g(x)\,dx}\]

The rules extend to any finite number of terms.

The Constant-Multiple Rule

STATEMENT

If F is an antiderivative of f, multiplying both functions by the same constant preserves that relationship.

CONSTANT-MULTIPLE RULE
\[\displaystyle \boxed{\int c f(x)\,dx=c\int f(x)\,dx}\]

The word constant matters: a factor involving x cannot be moved outside by this rule.

APPLICATION & VIDEO
● RECEXPAND
▶
CONCEPT VIDEO // CONSTANT FACTORS
EXAMPLE
\[\displaystyle \int7\cos x\,dx=7\int\cos x\,dx\]
\[\displaystyle \boxed{7\sin x+C}\]
Important: this rule cannot be used when the factor is x. The variable x is not a constant.

Linearity of Indefinite Integrals

LINEAR COMBINATION

Combine the sum rule and the constant-multiple rule.

LINEARITY
\[\displaystyle \boxed{\int(af(x)+bg(x))\,dx=a\int f(x)\,dx+b\int g(x)\,dx}\]

Here a and b are constants.

The integral of a linear combination is the same linear combination of the integrals.

WORKED EXAMPLE & VIDEO

\[\displaystyle \int(4x^3-5\sin x)dx\]
\[\displaystyle =4\left(\frac{x^4}{4}\right)-5(-\cos x)+C\]

Why Only One Final +C?

THE CONSTANTS COMBINE

Separating an integral appears to create two constants:

SEPARATED FAMILY
\[\displaystyle F(x)+C_1+G(x)+C_2\]

But C₁ + C₂ is itself an arbitrary constant.

RENAME THE CONSTANT
\[\displaystyle C_1+C_2=C\]

The same idea applies to C₁ − C₂ and constant multiples.

FINAL FORM & VIDEO
● RECEXPAND
▶
CONCEPT VIDEO // THE FINAL +C
WRITE ONE CONSTANT
\[\displaystyle \boxed{\int(f+g)dx=F(x)+G(x)+C}\]
Do not attach a different arbitrary constant to every term in the final answer.

There Is No Product Property

FALSE STATEMENT

Linearity applies to sums, differences, and constant multiples. It does not apply to products.

DO NOT USE
\[\displaystyle \boxed{\int f(x)g(x)\,dx\ne\left(\int f(x)\,dx\right)\left(\int g(x)\,dx\right)}\]

This is the equation crossed out in the lecture screenshot.

COUNTEREXAMPLE & VIDEO

WARNING VIDEO // NO PRODUCT RULE
LET f(x)=g(x)=x
\[\displaystyle \int x^2dx=\frac{x^3}{3}+C\]
But the false rule would give
\[\displaystyle \left(\int xdx\right)^2=\left(\frac{x^2}{2}\right)^2=\frac{x^4}{4}.\]
Their derivatives are x² and x³, so they cannot be the same family.
