Fundamental Theorem of Calculus and Definite Integrals

1. From Accumulation to Antiderivatives

A definite integral measures signed accumulation. If \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\), then

\[ \int_a^b f(x)\,dx \]

is the net signed area between the graph of \(f\) and the \(x\)-axis from \(x=a\) to \(x=b\). Area above the axis contributes positively, while area below the axis contributes negatively.

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus connects this geometric idea to antiderivatives. It says that differentiation and integration are inverse processes.

ACCUMULATION FUNCTION

Given a continuous function \(f\), define

\[ F(x)=\int_a^x f(t)\,dt. \]

The value \(F(x)\) is the net accumulation of \(f\) from the fixed starting point \(a\) to the variable endpoint \(x\). The letter \(t\) is a dummy variable; it may be replaced by any other unused variable.

2. Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, Part 1

Part 1 explains how to differentiate an accumulation function.

FTC PART 1: DERIVATIVE OF AN ACCUMULATION FUNCTION

If \(f\) is continuous on an interval containing \(a\) and \(x\), then

\[ \boxed{\frac{d}{dx}\int_a^x f(t)\,dt=f(x)}. \]

More generally, if the upper limit is a differentiable function \(g(x)\), then

\[ \boxed{\frac{d}{dx}\int_a^{g(x)} f(t)\,dt=f\bigl(g(x)\bigr)g'(x)}. \]

The second formula is FTC Part 1 followed by the chain rule.

EXAMPLE 1: DIFFERENTIATING AN ACCUMULATION FUNCTION

Let \[ G(x)=\int_2^x \sqrt{1+t^4}\,dt. \]

Find \(G'(x)\).

By FTC Part 1, replace the dummy variable \(t\) by the upper limit \(x\):

\[ \boxed{G'(x)=\sqrt{1+x^4}}. \]

No antiderivative of \(\sqrt{1+t^4}\) is needed.

EXAMPLE 2: FTC WITH THE CHAIN RULE

Find the derivative of \[ H(x)=\int_1^{x^3}\cos(t^2)\,dt. \]

The outer function contributes \(\cos((x^3)^2)\) and the upper limit contributes its derivative:

\[ \boxed{H'(x)=\cos(x^6)\cdot 3x^2}. \]
WARNING // VARIABLE LIMITS: WATCH THE SIGN

If the variable expression occurs in the lower limit, reverse the limits first:

\[ \frac{d}{dx}\int_{g(x)}^a f(t)\,dt =-f\bigl(g(x)\bigr)g'(x). \]

With two variable limits,

\[ \boxed{\frac{d}{dx}\int_{u(x)}^{v(x)}f(t)\,dt =f(v(x))v'(x)-f(u(x))u'(x)}. \]

3. Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, Part 2

Part 2 provides an efficient method for evaluating a definite integral.

FTC PART 2: EVALUATION THEOREM

If \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\) and \(F\) is any antiderivative of \(f\), then

\[ \boxed{\int_a^b f(x)\,dx=F(b)-F(a)}. \]

This is often written as

\[ \int_a^b f(x)\,dx=\left[F(x)\right]_a^b. \]
EXAMPLE 3: EVALUATING A DEFINITE INTEGRAL

Evaluate \[ \int_1^3\left(2x+\frac{1}{x}\right)dx. \]

An antiderivative is \(F(x)=x^2+\ln|x|\). Therefore,

\[ \begin{aligned} \int_1^3\left(2x+\frac{1}{x}\right)dx &=\left[x^2+\ln|x|\right]_1^3\\ &=(9+\ln 3)-(1+\ln 1)\\ &=\boxed{8+\ln 3}. \end{aligned} \]
WARNING // DEFINITE INTEGRALS DO NOT INCLUDE +C

The constant of integration is used for an indefinite integral:

\[ \int f(x)\,dx=F(x)+C. \]

For a definite integral, any constant cancels in \(F(b)-F(a)\), so do not write \(+C\) in the final answer.

4. Properties of Definite Integrals

For integrable functions \(f\) and \(g\) and a constant \(k\):

ESSENTIAL PROPERTIES
\[ \begin{aligned} \int_a^a f(x)\,dx&=0,\\[4pt] \int_a^b f(x)\,dx&=-\int_b^a f(x)\,dx,\\[4pt] \int_a^b kf(x)\,dx&=k\int_a^b f(x)\,dx,\\[4pt] \int_a^b [f(x)\pm g(x)]\,dx &=\int_a^b f(x)\,dx\pm\int_a^b g(x)\,dx,\\[4pt] \int_a^b f(x)\,dx+\int_b^c f(x)\,dx&=\int_a^c f(x)\,dx. \end{aligned} \]
EXAMPLE 4: USING KNOWN INTEGRAL VALUES

Suppose \[ \int_1^4 f(x)\,dx=7 \qquad\text{and}\qquad \int_4^6 f(x)\,dx=-2. \]

Then \[ \int_1^6 f(x)\,dx=7+(-2)=\boxed{5}, \]

and \[ \int_6^1 f(x)\,dx=-\int_1^6 f(x)\,dx=\boxed{-5}. \]

5. Net Area Versus Total Area

The definite integral gives net signed area, not necessarily the total geometric area. To find total area, split the interval wherever \(f(x)=0\) and make every contribution positive.

NET AREA AND TOTAL AREA
\[ \text{Net area}=\int_a^b f(x)\,dx, \qquad \text{Total area}=\int_a^b |f(x)|\,dx. \]

Equivalently, split at each \(x\)-intercept and subtract the integral on every interval where \(f(x)<0\).

EXAMPLE 5: SIGNED AREA

Evaluate \(\displaystyle\int_{-1}^{2}(x-1)\,dx\) and find the total area between \(y=x-1\) and the \(x\)-axis.

The net signed area is

\[ \int_{-1}^{2}(x-1)\,dx =\left[\frac{x^2}{2}-x\right]_{-1}^{2} =\boxed{-\frac12}. \]

Since the graph crosses the axis at \(x=1\), the total area is

\[ \begin{aligned} \int_{-1}^{2}|x-1|\,dx &=-\int_{-1}^{1}(x-1)\,dx+\int_{1}^{2}(x-1)\,dx\\[3pt] &=2+\frac12=\boxed{\frac52}. \end{aligned} \]

7. Strategy Summary

HOW TO RECOGNIZE THE CORRECT METHOD
  1. If asked to differentiate an integral with a variable limit, use FTC Part 1 and the chain rule.
  2. If asked to evaluate a definite integral, find an antiderivative and compute upper value minus lower value.
  3. If the limits are reversed, introduce a negative sign.
  4. Split integrals when an interval is broken into pieces or when a graph crosses the \(x\)-axis.
  5. Distinguish net change from total accumulation: total accumulation often requires an absolute value.
WARNING // COMMON ERRORS
  • Forgetting the chain-rule factor from a variable endpoint.
  • Using the same symbol for the endpoint and the dummy variable.
  • Computing \(F(a)-F(b)\) instead of \(F(b)-F(a)\).
  • Adding \(+C\) to a definite integral.
  • Treating signed area below the axis as positive area.
