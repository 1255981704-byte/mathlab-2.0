Evaluating and Applying Definite Integrals

1. Evaluating Definite Integrals

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus turns a definite integral into an antiderivative problem. If \(F'(x)=f(x)\), then

\[ \int_a^b f(x)\,dx=F(b)-F(a). \]

The result is a number representing net accumulation over \([a,b]\).

EVALUATION PROCEDURE

To evaluate \(\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)\,dx\):

  1. Find an antiderivative \(F\) of \(f\).
  2. Write \(\left[F(x)\right]_a^b\).
  3. Substitute the upper and lower limits: \(F(b)-F(a)\).
  4. Simplify and include units when the integral represents a quantity.
EXAMPLE 1: EXACT EVALUATION

Evaluate \[ \int_0^2(3x^2-4x+1)\,dx. \]

An antiderivative is \(x^3-2x^2+x\), so

\[ \begin{aligned} \int_0^2(3x^2-4x+1)\,dx &=\left[x^3-2x^2+x\right]_0^2\\ &=(8-8+2)-0=\boxed{2}. \end{aligned} \]
WARNING // BEFORE INTEGRATING, SIMPLIFY

Algebra can reveal a familiar antiderivative. For example,

\[ \int_1^4\frac{x^2+2x}{x}\,dx =\int_1^4(x+2)\,dx. \]

Always check whether the integrand can be expanded, factored, or reduced first.

2. Symmetry and Geometry

Some definite integrals can be evaluated without finding an antiderivative. Symmetry is especially useful on intervals of the form \([-a,a]\).

EVEN AND ODD FUNCTIONS

If \(f\) is even, so that \(f(-x)=f(x)\), then

\[ \boxed{\int_{-a}^{a}f(x)\,dx=2\int_0^a f(x)\,dx}. \]

If \(f\) is odd, so that \(f(-x)=-f(x)\), then

\[ \boxed{\int_{-a}^{a}f(x)\,dx=0}. \]
EXAMPLE 2: USING SYMMETRY

Evaluate \[ \int_{-2}^{2}(x^3+4)\,dx. \]

The term \(x^3\) is odd and its integral over \([-2,2]\) is zero. The constant term is even, so

\[ \int_{-2}^{2}(x^3+4)\,dx=0+4(2-(-2))=\boxed{16}. \]
EXAMPLE 3: EVALUATION FROM GEOMETRY

Evaluate \[ \int_{-3}^{3}\sqrt{9-x^2}\,dx. \]

The graph \(y=\sqrt{9-x^2}\) is the upper semicircle of radius \(3\). Therefore,

\[ \int_{-3}^{3}\sqrt{9-x^2}\,dx =\frac12\pi(3)^2=\boxed{\frac{9\pi}{2}}. \]

3. Average Value of a Function

The average value of a continuous function is the height of a rectangle whose area equals the signed area under the function over the same interval.

AVERAGE VALUE

If \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\), its average value is

\[ \boxed{f_{\mathrm{avg}}=\frac{1}{b-a}\int_a^b f(x)\,dx}. \]

The factor \(b-a\) is the length of the interval.

EXAMPLE 4: FINDING AN AVERAGE VALUE

Find the average value of \(f(x)=x^2+1\) on \([0,3]\).

\[ \begin{aligned} f_{\mathrm{avg}} &=\frac{1}{3-0}\int_0^3(x^2+1)\,dx\\ &=\frac13\left[\frac{x^3}{3}+x\right]_0^3 =\frac13(9+3)=\boxed{4}. \end{aligned} \]
MEAN VALUE THEOREM FOR INTEGRALS

If \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\), then there is at least one number \(c\) in \([a,b]\) such that

\[ f(c)=\frac{1}{b-a}\int_a^b f(x)\,dx. \]

In other words, the function attains its average value somewhere on the interval.

4. The Net Change Theorem

If a rate of change is known, integrating the rate gives the total change in the original quantity.

NET CHANGE THEOREM

If \(Q'(t)\) is the rate of change of a quantity \(Q\), then

\[ \boxed{\int_a^b Q'(t)\,dt=Q(b)-Q(a)}. \]

Therefore,

\[ \boxed{Q(b)=Q(a)+\int_a^b Q'(t)\,dt}. \]
EXAMPLE 5: RECOVERING A QUANTITY FROM ITS RATE

Water enters a tank at the rate \[ r(t)=6+2t \quad \text{liters per minute}, \] where \(0\leq t\leq 5\). If the tank initially contains \(40\) liters, how much water is in the tank after \(5\) minutes?

\[ \begin{aligned} Q(5)&=Q(0)+\int_0^5 r(t)\,dt\\ &=40+\left[6t+t^2\right]_0^5\\ &=40+30+25=\boxed{95\text{ liters}}. \end{aligned} \]
WARNING // UNITS OF AN INTEGRAL

Multiply the units of the integrand by the units of the variable of integration. A rate measured in liters per minute, integrated with respect to minutes, produces liters. This quick check often catches an incorrect setup.

5. Motion: Displacement and Total Distance

For motion along a line, velocity is the rate of change of position: \(v(t)=s'(t)\). Integrating velocity gives displacement.

MOTION FORMULAS
\[ \boxed{\text{Displacement}=\int_a^b v(t)\,dt=s(b)-s(a)} \] \[ \boxed{\text{Total distance}=\int_a^b |v(t)|\,dt} \]

The particle changes direction whenever \(v(t)\) changes sign.

EXAMPLE 6: DISPLACEMENT VERSUS DISTANCE

A particle moves with velocity \[ v(t)=t^2-4t+3=(t-1)(t-3), \qquad 0\leq t\leq4. \] Find its displacement and total distance traveled.

The displacement is

\[ \int_0^4 v(t)\,dt =\left[\frac{t^3}{3}-2t^2+3t\right]_0^4 =\boxed{\frac43}. \]

Velocity changes sign at \(t=1\) and \(t=3\). Thus the total distance is

\[ \begin{aligned} \int_0^4|v(t)|\,dt &=\int_0^1v(t)\,dt-\int_1^3v(t)\,dt+\int_3^4v(t)\,dt\\ &=\frac43+\frac43+\frac43=\boxed{4}. \end{aligned} \]

6. Accumulation from Data

When a rate is given by a table rather than a formula, a definite integral can be approximated with trapezoids. With unequal subintervals, compute each width separately.

EXAMPLE 7: APPLYING THE TRAPEZOIDAL RULE TO DATA

The inflow rate \(r(t)\), in gallons per minute, is recorded below.

\(t\) (min)0258
\(r(t)\) (gal/min)4759

Approximate the total amount of water added from \(t=0\) to \(t=8\):

\[ \begin{aligned} \int_0^8r(t)\,dt &\approx \frac{2}{2}(4+7)+\frac{3}{2}(7+5) +\frac{3}{2}(5+9)\\ &=11+18+21=\boxed{50\text{ gallons}}. \end{aligned} \]

8. Strategy Summary

CHOOSING THE RIGHT INTERPRETATION
  1. For an exact integral, find an antiderivative and evaluate upper minus lower.
  2. Before integrating, look for symmetry, familiar geometric regions, or algebraic simplifications.
  3. For average value, divide the integral by the interval length \(b-a\).
  4. To recover a quantity, add its initial value to the integral of its rate of change.
  5. In motion problems, integrate \(v\) for displacement and \(|v|\) for total distance.
  6. Check that the units of the answer match the quantity being requested.
WARNING // COMMON ERRORS
  • Forgetting the factor \(1/(b-a)\) in an average-value problem.
  • Confusing a rate with the accumulated amount.
  • Adding an initial value when the question asks only for net change.
  • Using displacement when the question asks for total distance.
  • Ignoring points where velocity changes sign.
