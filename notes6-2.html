DATA LOG // SECTION 6.2

Properties of Indefinite Integrals

1. Review and Motivation

If \(F'(x)=f(x)\), then

\[ \int f(x)\,dx=F(x)+C. \]

The properties in this section allow a complicated integrand to be separated into simpler pieces. They follow from the corresponding rules for differentiation.

THE CENTRAL IDEA: LINEARITY

Differentiation satisfies

\[ \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(aF(x)+bG(x)\bigr)=aF'(x)+bG'(x), \]

where \(a\) and \(b\) are constants. Reversing this statement gives the linearity properties of indefinite integration.

Linearity permits sums and differences to be integrated term by term and permits constant factors to be moved outside an integral.

2. Sum and Difference Properties

Suppose \(F'=f\) and \(G'=g\). Since

\[ \frac{d}{dx}(F+G)=f+g, \]

the function \(F+G\) is an antiderivative of \(f+g\).

SUM AND DIFFERENCE PROPERTIES
\[ \boxed{ \int\bigl(f(x)+g(x)\bigr)\,dx =\int f(x)\,dx+\int g(x)\,dx } \]

and

\[ \boxed{ \int\bigl(f(x)-g(x)\bigr)\,dx =\int f(x)\,dx-\int g(x)\,dx }. \]

These rules extend to any finite number of terms.

EXAMPLE 1: INTEGRATING A SUM

Evaluate \(\int(x^3+1/x)\,dx\).

\[ \begin{aligned} \int\left(x^3+\frac{1}{x}\right)\,dx &= \int x^3\,dx+\int\frac{1}{x}\,dx\\[3pt] &= \boxed{\frac{x^4}{4}+\ln|x|+C}. \end{aligned} \]

3. The Constant-Multiple Property

If \(F'=f\) and \(c\) is constant, then

\[ \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(cF(x)\bigr)=cF'(x)=cf(x). \]
CONSTANT-MULTIPLE PROPERTY

For every constant \(c\),

\[ \boxed{\int c f(x)\,dx=c\int f(x)\,dx}. \]

The arbitrary constant is understood to be included in the final answer.

EXAMPLE 2: MOVING A CONSTANT FACTOR
\[ \begin{aligned} \int 7\cos x\,dx &= 7\int\cos x\,dx\\[3pt] &= \boxed{7\sin x+C}. \end{aligned} \]
WARNING // A VARIABLE FACTOR IS NOT A CONSTANT

The expression \(\int x\cos x\,dx\) cannot be rewritten as \(x\int\cos x\,dx\), because \(x\) varies. Only a factor independent of the variable of integration may be moved outside.

4. General Linearity

The preceding properties combine into the formula emphasized in the lecture.

LINEARITY OF INDEFINITE INTEGRALS

For constants \(a\) and \(b\),

\[ \boxed{ \int\bigl(af(x)+bg(x)\bigr)\,dx =a\int f(x)\,dx+b\int g(x)\,dx }. \]

In words, the integral of a linear combination is the same linear combination of the integrals.

EXAMPLE 3: A LINEAR COMBINATION

Evaluate \(\int(4x^3-5\sin x)\,dx\).

\[ \begin{aligned} \int(4x^3-5\sin x)\,dx &= 4\int x^3\,dx-5\int\sin x\,dx\\[3pt] &= 4\left(\frac{x^4}{4}\right)-5(-\cos x)+C\\[3pt] &= \boxed{x^4+5\cos x+C}. \end{aligned} \]

5. Constants of Integration

Separating an integral may appear to produce several constants:

\[ \begin{aligned} \int(f+g)\,dx &= \int f\,dx+\int g\,dx\\ &= F(x)+C_1+G(x)+C_2. \end{aligned} \]

But \(C_1+C_2\) is itself an arbitrary constant, so it is renamed \(C\).

ONE CONSTANT IN THE FINAL ANSWER

After integrating term by term, write one arbitrary constant at the end:

\[ \boxed{\int(f(x)+g(x))\,dx=F(x)+G(x)+C}. \]

There is no need to attach a different constant to every term.

The same principle applies to \(C_1-C_2\) and to constant multiples: all arbitrary constants are absorbed into one final \(C\).

6. What Linearity Does Not Permit

Linearity concerns sums, differences, and constant multiples. It does not turn a product of functions into a product of integrals.

WARNING // NO PRODUCT PROPERTY

In general,

\[ \boxed{ \int f(x)g(x)\,dx \neq \left(\int f(x)\,dx\right) \left(\int g(x)\,dx\right) }. \]

The product equation crossed out in the lecture is false. Integration has no product rule of this form.

For a counterexample, let \(f(x)=g(x)=x\). Then

\[ \int x\cdot x\,dx=\int x^2\,dx=\frac{x^3}{3}+C, \]

whereas the proposed right side, ignoring constants, would be

\[ \left(\int x\,dx\right)^2 =\left(\frac{x^2}{2}\right)^2 =\frac{x^4}{4}. \]

These cannot differ merely by a constant because

\[ \frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{x^3}{3}\right)=x^2 \qquad\text{but}\qquad \frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{x^4}{4}\right)=x^3. \]

Thus the proposed product property fails.

7. Integration Term by Term

Rewrite radicals and reciprocal powers using exponents when this makes the power rule easier to apply.

EXAMPLE 4: POLYNOMIAL AND RECIPROCAL POWERS

Evaluate \[ \int\left(6x^5-3x^2+4-\frac{2}{x^3}\right)\,dx. \]

Since \(-2/x^3=-2x^{-3}\), linearity gives

\[ \begin{aligned} &\int\left(6x^5-3x^2+4-2x^{-3}\right)\,dx\\[3pt] &\quad=6\int x^5\,dx-3\int x^2\,dx +4\int 1\,dx-2\int x^{-3}\,dx\\[3pt] &\quad=6\left(\frac{x^6}{6}\right) -3\left(\frac{x^3}{3}\right)+4x -2\left(\frac{x^{-2}}{-2}\right)+C\\[3pt] &\quad=\boxed{x^6-x^3+4x+\frac{1}{x^2}+C}. \end{aligned} \]
EXAMPLE 5: SEVERAL BASIC FORMS

Evaluate \[ \int\left(3\sqrt{x}+\frac{5}{x}-2\cos x\right)\,dx. \]

Using \(\sqrt{x}=x^{1/2}\),

\[ \begin{aligned} &\int\left(3\sqrt{x}+\frac{5}{x}-2\cos x\right)\,dx\\[3pt] &\quad=3\int x^{1/2}\,dx+5\int\frac{1}{x}\,dx -2\int\cos x\,dx\\[3pt] &\quad=3\left(\frac{x^{3/2}}{3/2}\right) +5\ln|x|-2\sin x+C\\[3pt] &\quad=\boxed{2x^{3/2}+5\ln|x|-2\sin x+C}. \end{aligned} \]

8. Verification by Differentiation

Differentiate the answer to Example 5:

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{d}{dx}\left(2x^{3/2}+5\ln|x|-2\sin x+C\right) &=2\left(\frac{3}{2}\right)x^{1/2} +\frac{5}{x}-2\cos x\\[3pt] &=3\sqrt{x}+\frac{5}{x}-2\cos x. \end{aligned} \]

The derivative reproduces the original integrand, confirming the result.

WARNING // COMMON ERRORS
  • Do not move a factor outside unless it is constant.
  • Do not multiply separate integrals to integrate a product.
  • Do not forget the constant of integration \(C\).
  • Do not use the power rule for \(x^{-1}\); use \(\int x^{-1}\,dx=\ln|x|+C\).
  • Combine all arbitrary constants into one final constant \(C\).

9. Principal Results

PROPERTIES OF INDEFINITE INTEGRALS
\[ \boxed{ \int\bigl(af(x)+bg(x)\bigr)\,dx =a\int f(x)\,dx+b\int g(x)\,dx } \]

for constants \(a\) and \(b\). In particular,

\[ \begin{aligned} \int(f+g)\,dx&=\int f\,dx+\int g\,dx,\\[3pt] \int(f-g)\,dx&=\int f\,dx-\int g\,dx,\\[3pt] \int cf\,dx&=c\int f\,dx. \end{aligned} \]

Linearity makes it possible to integrate sums term by term. It does not apply to products of functions. Every final result should contain one constant of integration and should be verifiable by differentiation.
