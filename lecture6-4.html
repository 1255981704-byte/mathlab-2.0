Why Introduce a New Variable?

PROBLEM & STRUCTURE

Start with the lecture's first example:

PROBLEM
\[\int(2x+1)^3dx\]
\[(2x+1)^3=8x^3+12x^2+6x+1\]
\[=2x^4+4x^3+3x^2+x+C\]

Expansion works, but it scales badly for large powers.

SUBSTITUTION
\[u=2x+1\]
\[du=2dx\]
Substitution turns a composite power into an ordinary power of (u).

Track the Differential Carefully

SET UP & SOLUTION
CHOOSE U
\[u=2x+1\]
DIFFERENTIAL
\[\frac{du}{dx}=2\Rightarrow du=2dx\Rightarrow dx=\frac12du\]

Replace both the inner expression and the differential dx.

WORKED SOLUTION
\[\int u^3\frac12du=\frac12\cdot\frac{u^4}{4}+C\]
\[\boxed{\frac18(2x+1)^4+C}\]

Denominator and Its Derivative

PROBLEM & SOLUTION
INTEGRAL
\[\int\frac{x}{x^2+9}dx\]
WORKED SOLUTION
\[u=x^2+9,\qquad du=2x\,dx,\qquad x\,dx=\frac12du\]
\[\frac12\int\frac1u du=\frac12\ln|u|+C\]
\[\boxed{\frac12\ln(x^2+9)+C}\]
The denominator is always positive.

Different Forms, Same Family

IDENTITY & EQUIVALENCE
IDENTITY
\[\cos^2x=1-\sin^2x\]
\[-\frac12\cos^2x+C=-\frac12+\frac12\sin^2x+C\]
\[=\frac12\sin^2x+\widetilde C\]
BIG PICTURE
Indefinite integrals that differ only by a constant represent the same family.
Differentiate both answers: each derivative is (sin xcos x).

Rewrite Before Substituting

PROBLEM & SOLUTION
INTEGRAL
\[\int\frac{\sin^5x}{\cos^7x}dx\]
WORKED SOLUTION
\[\frac{\sin^5x}{\cos^7x}=\frac{\sin^5x}{\cos^5x}\frac1{\cos^2x}=\tan^5x\sec^2x\]
\[u=\tan x,\quad du=\sec^2x\,dx\]
\[\int u^5du=\boxed{\frac16\tan^6x+C}\]

Reverse the Chain Rule

CORE FORMULA
U-SUBSTITUTION
\[\boxed{\int f(g(x))g'(x)dx=F(g(x))+C}\]

Choose the inner expression as u, compute du, rewrite the entire integral, integrate, and substitute back.

FINAL CHECKLIST
Can you identify an inner function?
Is its derivative present up to a constant?
Did every (x) and (dx) disappear after substitution?
Did you substitute back and add (C)?
