DATA LOG // SECTION 6.4

Integration by Substitution

1. Why Introduce a New Variable?

The lecture begins with

\[ \int(2x+1)^3\,dx. \]

One valid method is to expand first:

\[ (2x+1)^3=8x^3+12x^2+6x+1. \]

Then

\[ \int(2x+1)^3dx =2x^4+4x^3+3x^2+x+C. \]

This works, but expansion becomes inefficient for higher powers or more complicated inner functions.

THE STRUCTURAL OBSERVATION

The integrand is a power of the inner function \(2x+1\). The derivative of that inner function is the constant \(2\):

\[ \frac{d}{dx}(2x+1)=2. \]

Substitution temporarily names the inner function \(u\), allowing the ordinary power rule to be applied directly.

2. Example 1: The Lecture's First Substitution

Evaluate \[ \int(2x+1)^3\,dx. \]

Let \[ u=2x+1. \]

Because \(u\) is a function of \(x\), differentiate both sides:

\[ \frac{du}{dx}=2 \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad du=2\,dx \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad dx=\frac12\,du. \]

Now replace both the inner function and the differential:

\[ \begin{aligned} \int(2x+1)^3\,dx &=\int u^3\left(\frac12\,du\right)\\[3pt] &=\frac12\int u^3\,du\\[3pt] &=\frac12\cdot\frac{u^4}{4}+C\\[3pt] &=\frac18u^4+C. \end{aligned} \]

Substitute back:

\[ \boxed{\int(2x+1)^3\,dx=\frac18(2x+1)^4+C}. \]
THE SUBSTITUTION ALGORITHM
  1. Choose \(u\) as the useful inner expression.
  2. Compute \(du\) from \(du/dx\).
  3. Solve for the factor involving \(dx\), if necessary.
  4. Rewrite the entire integral using only \(u\) and \(du\).
  5. Integrate, then substitute back to \(x\).

3. Example 2: A Denominator and Its Derivative

Evaluate \[ \int\frac{x}{x^2+9}\,dx. \]

The lecture highlights the denominator as the inner function and the numerator as part of its derivative. Let

\[ u=x^2+9, \qquad du=2x\,dx, \qquad x\,dx=\frac12\,du. \]

Therefore,

\[ \begin{aligned} \int\frac{x}{x^2+9}\,dx &=\frac12\int\frac1u\,du\\[3pt] &=\frac12\ln|u|+C\\[3pt] &=\boxed{\frac12\ln(x^2+9)+C}. \end{aligned} \]

Absolute values are unnecessary in the final line because \(x^2+9>0\).

LOGARITHMIC PATTERN
\[ \boxed{\int\frac{g'(x)}{g(x)}\,dx=\ln|g(x)|+C}. \]

If only a constant multiple of \(g'(x)\) is present, adjust that constant outside the integral.

4. Example 3: Two Valid Choices of \(u\)

Evaluate \[ \int\sin x\cos x\,dx. \]

The lecture gives two correct substitutions.

SOLUTION 1: CHOOSE SINE FOR THE NEW VARIABLE

Then \(du=\cos x\,dx\), so

\[ \begin{aligned} \int\sin x\cos x\,dx &=\int u\,du\\[3pt] &=\frac12u^2+C\\[3pt] &=\boxed{\frac12\sin^2x+C}. \end{aligned} \]
SOLUTION 2: CHOOSE COSINE FOR THE NEW VARIABLE

Then \(du=-\sin x\,dx\), so

\[ \begin{aligned} \int\sin x\cos x\,dx &=-\int u\,du\\[3pt] &=-\frac12u^2+C\\[3pt] &=\boxed{-\frac12\cos^2x+C}. \end{aligned} \]

The two answers appear different, but

\[ \begin{aligned} -\frac12\cos^2x+C &=-\frac12(1-\sin^2x)+C\\[3pt] &=\frac12\sin^2x+\left(C-\frac12\right). \end{aligned} \]

Since \(C-1/2\) is still an arbitrary constant, the two answers represent the same family of antiderivatives.

EQUIVALENT ANTIDERIVATIVES

Two correct indefinite-integral answers may look different. If they differ by only a constant, they represent the same family. Differentiate each answer to confirm that both produce the original integrand.

5. Example 4: Rewrite Before Substituting

Evaluate the lecture's final example:

\[ \int\frac{\sin^5x}{\cos^7x}\,dx. \]

The visible pattern is not yet a direct substitution. Use

\[ \tan x=\frac{\sin x}{\cos x}, \qquad \sec^2x=\frac1{\cos^2x}. \]

Rewrite:

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{\sin^5x}{\cos^7x} &=\frac{\sin^5x}{\cos^5x}\cdot\frac1{\cos^2x}\\[3pt] &=\tan^5x\sec^2x. \end{aligned} \]

Now let

\[ u=\tan x, \qquad du=\sec^2x\,dx. \]

Then

\[ \begin{aligned} \int\frac{\sin^5x}{\cos^7x}\,dx &=\int u^5\,du\\[3pt] &=\frac16u^6+C\\[3pt] &=\boxed{\frac16\tan^6x+C}. \end{aligned} \]

6. How to Choose \(u\)

PATTERN CHECKLIST

Look for one of the following structures:

  • a power of an inner expression together with its derivative;
  • a denominator together with its derivative in the numerator;
  • a trigonometric function together with the differential of another;
  • an expression that becomes a power after an identity or algebraic rewrite.
WARNING // COMMON ERRORS
  • Replacing the inner expression but leaving \(dx\) unchanged.
  • Forgetting the factor \(1/2\) when \(du=2\,dx\) or \(du=2x\,dx\).
  • Mixing \(x\) and \(u\) in the transformed integral.
  • Losing the negative sign when \(u=\cos x\).
  • Assuming two antiderivatives are different without checking whether they differ only by a constant.

7. Principal Results

REVERSE CHAIN RULE

If \(F'=f\), then

\[ \boxed{\int f(g(x))g'(x)\,dx=F(g(x))+C}. \]
