DATA LOG // SECTION 6.1

Antiderivatives and Indefinite Integrals

1. Antiderivatives

Antidifferentiation is the inverse process of differentiation. Given a function \(f\), antidifferentiation determines a function whose derivative is \(f\).

DEFINITION OF AN ANTIDERIVATIVE

Let \(f\) be defined on an interval \(I\). A function \(F\) is an antiderivative of \(f\) on \(I\) if

\[ F'(x)=f(x) \]

for every \(x\in I\).

For example, \[ \frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{x^2}{2}\right)=x. \]

Therefore, the function \[ F(x)=\frac{x^2}{2} \] is an antiderivative of \(f(x)=x\).

The validity of a proposed antiderivative is established by differentiation. If differentiating \(F\) produces \(f\), then \(F\) is an antiderivative of \(f\).

2. The General Antiderivative

The derivative of a constant is zero. Consequently, adding a constant to a function does not change its derivative. For example,

\[ \frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{x^2}{2}\right)=x, \]

while \[ \frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{x^2}{2}+1\right)=x \] and \[ \frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{x^2}{2}+100\right)=x. \]

More generally, \[ \frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{x^2}{2}+C\right)=x \] for every constant \(C\).

GENERAL ANTIDERIVATIVE THEOREM

Suppose that \(F\) is an antiderivative of \(f\) on an interval \(I\). Every antiderivative of \(f\) on \(I\) has the form

\[ F(x)+C, \]

where \(C\) is an arbitrary constant.

The expression \(F(x)+C\) is called the general antiderivative of \(f\). The constant \(C\) is called the constant of integration.

For the function \(f(x)=x\), the general antiderivative is \[ \frac{x^2}{2}+C. \]

The interval condition in the theorem ensures that any two antiderivatives of the same function differ by a constant.

3. Indefinite-Integral Notation

The family of all antiderivatives of a function is represented by an indefinite integral.

INDEFINITE INTEGRAL

If \(F'(x)=f(x)\), then

\[ \boxed{\int f(x)\,dx=F(x)+C}. \]

In this notation:

  • \(\int\) is the integral sign;
  • \(f(x)\) is the integrand;
  • \(dx\) specifies the variable of integration; and
  • \(C\) is the constant of integration.

Since \[ \frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{x^2}{2}\right)=x, \] the corresponding indefinite integral is \[ \boxed{\int x\,dx=\frac{x^2}{2}+C}. \]

An indefinite integral denotes a family of functions rather than a single function. The equality \[ \int f(x)\,dx=F(x)+C \] states that every function in the family \(F(x)+C\) has derivative \(f(x)\).

EXAMPLE 1: INTEGRAL OF A CONSTANT

Evaluate \[ \int -2\,dx. \]

Since \[ \frac{d}{dx}(-2x)=-2, \] it follows that \[ \boxed{\int -2\,dx=-2x+C}. \]

EXAMPLE 2: INTEGRAL OF COSINE

Evaluate \[ \int\cos x\,dx. \]

The differentiation formula \[ \frac{d}{dx}(\sin x)=\cos x \] gives \[ \boxed{\int\cos x\,dx=\sin x+C}. \]

EXAMPLE 3: INTEGRAL OF SINE

Evaluate \[ \int\sin x\,dx. \]

Because \[ \frac{d}{dx}(-\cos x)=\sin x, \] the result is \[ \boxed{\int\sin x\,dx=-\cos x+C}. \]

4. Basic Integration Formulas

The basic integration formulas follow directly from the corresponding differentiation formulas.

CONSTANT RULE

For every constant \(a\),

\[ \boxed{\int a\,dx=ax+C}. \]
POWER RULE FOR INTEGRATION

For every real number \(n\neq -1\),

\[ \boxed{ \int x^n\,dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}+C }. \]

The power rule is verified by differentiation: \[ \frac{d}{dx} \left( \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} \right) = \frac{1}{n+1}(n+1)x^n = x^n. \]

The restriction \(n\neq -1\) is necessary because the expression \(\frac{1}{n+1}\) is undefined when \(n=-1\).

The case \(n=-1\) is governed by the logarithmic formula.

LOGARITHMIC FORMULA

On any interval that does not contain \(0\),

\[ \boxed{ \int\frac{1}{x}\,dx = \ln|x|+C }. \]

The elementary trigonometric formulas are \[ \boxed{\int\cos x\,dx=\sin x+C} \] and \[ \boxed{\int\sin x\,dx=-\cos x+C}. \]

EXAMPLE 4: APPLICATION OF THE POWER RULE

Evaluate \[ \int x^5\,dx. \]

Increasing the exponent by one and dividing by the resulting exponent gives

\[ \begin{aligned} \int x^5\,dx &= \frac{x^{5+1}}{5+1}+C \\[3pt] &= \boxed{\frac{x^6}{6}+C}. \end{aligned} \]

Verification: \[ \frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{x^6}{6}+C\right) = \frac{1}{6}(6x^5) = x^5. \]

5. Linearity of Indefinite Integrals

Indefinite integration is a linear operation. Sums and differences may be integrated term by term, and constant factors may be placed outside the integral.

SUM AND DIFFERENCE RULES

If \(f\) and \(g\) possess antiderivatives on an interval, then

\[ \boxed{ \int\bigl(f(x)+g(x)\bigr)\,dx = \int f(x)\,dx+\int g(x)\,dx } \]

and

\[ \boxed{ \int\bigl(f(x)-g(x)\bigr)\,dx = \int f(x)\,dx-\int g(x)\,dx }. \]
CONSTANT-MULTIPLE RULE

For every constant \(c\),

\[ \boxed{ \int c f(x)\,dx = c\int f(x)\,dx }. \]

Combining these properties gives the general linearity formula: \[ \boxed{ \int\bigl(af(x)+bg(x)\bigr)\,dx = a\int f(x)\,dx + b\int g(x)\,dx }, \] where \(a\) and \(b\) are constants.

When an integral is separated into several terms, only one arbitrary constant is required in the final expression. Indeed, a sum of arbitrary constants is itself an arbitrary constant: \[ C_1+C_2+\cdots+C_n=C. \]

WARNING // PRODUCTS OF FUNCTIONS

Linearity applies to sums, differences, and constant multiples. It does not apply to products of functions. In general,

\[ \boxed{ \int f(x)g(x)\,dx \neq \left(\int f(x)\,dx\right) \left(\int g(x)\,dx\right) }. \]

Thus, integration has no product rule of this form.

6. Integration Term by Term

EXAMPLE 5: A POLYNOMIAL AND RATIONAL EXPRESSION

Evaluate \[ \int \left( 3x^5+4x^4+2x^2+1+\frac{8}{x}-\frac{5}{x^2} \right)\,dx. \]

First, rewrite the final term using a negative exponent: \[ -\frac{5}{x^2}=-5x^{-2}. \]

The linearity properties give

\[ \begin{aligned} &\int \left( 3x^5+4x^4+2x^2+1+\frac{8}{x}-\frac{5}{x^2} \right)\,dx \\[4pt] &\quad= 3\int x^5\,dx + 4\int x^4\,dx + 2\int x^2\,dx + \int 1\,dx + 8\int\frac{1}{x}\,dx - 5\int x^{-2}\,dx. \end{aligned} \]

Applying the appropriate integration formula to each term yields

\[ \begin{aligned} &= 3\left(\frac{x^6}{6}\right) + 4\left(\frac{x^5}{5}\right) + 2\left(\frac{x^3}{3}\right) + x + 8\ln|x| - 5\left(\frac{x^{-1}}{-1}\right) + C\\[4pt] &= \frac{x^6}{2} + \frac{4x^5}{5} + \frac{2x^3}{3} + x + 8\ln|x| + \frac{5}{x} + C. \end{aligned} \]

Therefore,

\[ \boxed{ \begin{aligned} &\int \left( 3x^5+4x^4+2x^2+1+\frac{8}{x}-\frac{5}{x^2} \right)\,dx \\[3pt] &\qquad= \frac{x^6}{2} + \frac{4x^5}{5} + \frac{2x^3}{3} + x + 8\ln|x| + \frac{5}{x} + C. \end{aligned} } \]

The final term satisfies \[ \frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{5}{x}\right) = -\frac{5}{x^2}, \] which confirms its sign.

7. Linear Inner Functions

The chain rule introduces the derivative of an inner function. Reversing the chain rule therefore requires the corresponding constant factor to be taken into account.

EXAMPLE 6: INTEGRAL OF \(\sin(2x)\)

Evaluate \[ \int\sin(2x)\,dx. \]

The chain rule gives \[ \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(\cos(2x)\bigr) = -2\sin(2x). \]

Multiplication by \(-\frac12\) produces the required derivative:

\[ \frac{d}{dx} \left( -\frac12\cos(2x) \right) = -\frac12\bigl(-2\sin(2x)\bigr) = \sin(2x). \]

Consequently, \[ \boxed{ \int\sin(2x)\,dx = -\frac12\cos(2x)+C }. \]

More generally, for every nonzero constant \(a\),

\[ \boxed{ \int\sin(ax)\,dx = -\frac{1}{a}\cos(ax)+C } \]

and

\[ \boxed{ \int\cos(ax)\,dx = \frac{1}{a}\sin(ax)+C }. \]

These formulas follow from \[ \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(\cos(ax)\bigr) = -a\sin(ax) \] and \[ \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(\sin(ax)\bigr) = a\cos(ax). \]

8. Principal Results

SUMMARY OF FUNDAMENTAL FORMULAS
\[ \begin{aligned} \int a\,dx &=ax+C,\\[4pt] \int x^n\,dx &=\frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}+C, &&n\neq -1,\\[4pt] \int\frac{1}{x}\,dx &=\ln|x|+C,\\[4pt] \int\cos x\,dx &=\sin x+C,\\[4pt] \int\sin x\,dx &=-\cos x+C,\\[4pt] \int\cos(ax)\,dx &=\frac{1}{a}\sin(ax)+C, &&a\neq 0,\\[4pt] \int\sin(ax)\,dx &=-\frac{1}{a}\cos(ax)+C, &&a\neq 0. \end{aligned} \]

An antiderivative is an individual function \(F\) satisfying \(F'=f\). The indefinite integral

\[ \int f(x)\,dx=F(x)+C \]

represents the complete family of antiderivatives of \(f\) on an interval.

Every indefinite integral may be verified by differentiating the resulting general antiderivative.
